The wrong abstraction

Text models start from language already parsed.

Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya describes four levels of speech — from the silent source to the spoken word. Cognition moves through them; a text model skips straight to the last one. Śabda-ALM walks the whole path, and its architecture is these four levels.

परा

Parā

The source — the model's learned Sanskrit prior, silent and whole.

पश्यन्ती

Paśyantī · sphoṭa

The flash of meaning. The mid-network layer where sound has become sense.

मध्यमा

Madhyamā

Inner articulation — the projector that turns continuous sound into meaning-units.

वैखरी

Vaikharī

The uttered word — audio in, and audio out.

The architecture

Sound routed through a Sanskrit mind.

A frozen NVIDIA Parakeet encoder hears the audio; a trainable Sphoṭa Projector turns its frames into tokens the Sanskrit core understands; the core reasons; and neural speech turns the answer back into sound. Only the projector (and a few adapter weights) ever learn.

The discovery · the Sphoṭa-Lens

We can watch sound turn into meaning.

At every layer we ask: how well can the sentence's meaning be read from the sound positions alone? The answer rises to a peak, then fades toward the output. That peak — layer 13 — is the computational sphoṭa: where the flash of meaning happens. A causal test (ablating each layer) agrees. Meaning is decodable there at 11× chance.
