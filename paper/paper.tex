\begin{abstract}
Text language models begin from already-segmented symbols. Bhartṛhari's \emph{Vākyapadīya}
(5th c.), the neuroscience of inner speech, and the frontier of speech language models converge on
a different starting point: cognition begins as a continuous acoustic field that bursts into
discrete meaning (\emph{sphoṭa}), grasped through an intermediate representation
(\emph{madhyamā}/\emph{paśyantī}) before it is uttered (\emph{vaikharī}). We build \textbf{Śabda-ALM},
a speech-to-speech Audio Language Model that routes acoustic evidence from a frozen NVIDIA Parakeet
encoder, through a trainable \emph{Sphoṭa Projector}, into a from-scratch Sanskrit \emph{byte-core}
(a Nemotron-H/Mamba-2 hybrid), and back out through neural TTS. Two results follow. First, on a
held-out audio$\to$Sanskrit-text task the Śabda-ALM (CER $0.546$) \emph{outperforms} NVIDIA's SOTA
Parakeet-TDT native ASR head ($0.605$, same encoder) and OpenAI Whisper ($0.747$): routing sound
through the Sanskrit core reads Sanskrit speech better than a general decoder. Adapting the frozen
core with $1.5$M LoRA parameters lifts a $200$M model past a $1.13$B projector-only model. Second,
we introduce the \textbf{Sphoṭa-Lens}, an instrument that locates, correlationally and causally, the
layer at which the sentence's meaning becomes decodable from the audio positions alone. On the
trained model this is \emph{layer 13} of $25$ (decodability $0.25$ vs $0.022$ chance; correlational
and causal peaks agree within one layer): sound-borne meaning crystallises mid-network, then moves
toward articulation---the \emph{paśyantī}$\to$\emph{vaikharī} gradient made measurable. All results
are reproduced by machine-checkable dual-verdict gates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Modern language models operate over discrete text tokens---language that has \emph{already} been
parsed. The Indian grammarian Bhartṛhari argued that this inverts the order of cognition: awareness
is bathed in a continuous field of sound (\emph{dhvani}), and meaning arrives as \emph{sphoṭa}, an
indivisible ``burst'' that is not the sum of its acoustic parts. The tradition organises this into
four levels of speech (\emph{vāk}): \emph{parā} (the source), \emph{paśyantī} (the flash of meaning,
\emph{pratibhā}), \emph{madhyamā} (mental articulation), and \emph{vaikharī} (uttered sound).
Independently, the neuroscience of inner speech and the current frontier of speech language models
both move away from discrete text toward continuous acoustic representations.

We take this convergence as an \emph{engineering} directive rather than a metaphor, and ask: can a
speech-centred model built on a Sanskrit core (a) read Sanskrit speech better than general
SOTA systems, and (b) expose, as a measurable internal locus, the point where sound becomes meaning?
We answer both affirmatively, and contribute:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Śabda-ALM} (\S\ref{sec:arch}): a speech-to-speech ALM built by injecting projected
  acoustic embeddings into a frozen from-scratch Sanskrit byte-core via its native embedding path,
  proven faithful (identical predictions to the core's own forward).
  \item A \textbf{SOTA comparison} (\S\ref{sec:results}) in which Śabda-ALM ranks first against
  Parakeet-TDT and Whisper on the Sanskrit-target task, and a demonstration that LoRA adaptation of
  the core is a stronger lever than raw parameter scaling.
  \item The \textbf{Sphoṭa-Lens} (\S\ref{sec:lens}), a correlational-plus-causal instrument that
  localises meaning-emergence-from-sound to a validated mid-network layer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\textbf{Sphoṭa and the four \emph{vāk}.} Bhartṛhari's \emph{Vākyapadīya} treats the sentence-meaning
as \emph{pratibhā}, a distinct cognition arising when word-meanings are grasped together (VP 2.143),
not their running sum. We operationalise \emph{paśyantī}/sphoṭa as an internal representation in
which meaning is maximally present, and \emph{vaikharī} as the surface (byte) output.
\textbf{Audio language models.} SALMONN, Qwen2-Audio and NVIDIA's canary-qwen adopt a common
recipe: a frozen audio encoder, a trainable projector, and a causal LLM. We follow this recipe but
make the LLM a \emph{Sanskrit} byte-core, and add an interpretability instrument.
\textbf{Global-workspace lenses.} The Jacobian-lens / j-space line locates a text-only workspace
band via inter-layer representational similarity. Our Sphoṭa-Lens instead measures, per layer, the
\emph{decodability of meaning from the audio positions}, and validates the locus causally by
ablation---an audio-native measure the text-only lens cannot express.

\section{The Śabda-ALM Architecture}\label{sec:arch}
The model realises the four \emph{vāk} as a pipeline:
\[
\underbrace{\text{audio}}_{\text{vaikharī in}}
\xrightarrow{\text{Parakeet (frozen)}} \underbrace{\text{frames}}_{\text{dhvani}}
\xrightarrow{\text{Sphoṭa Projector}} \underbrace{\text{tokens}}_{\text{madhyamā}}
\xrightarrow{\text{Sanskrit byte-core}} \underbrace{\text{workspace}}_{\text{paśyantī/sphoṭa}}
\xrightarrow{} \underbrace{\text{text}\to\text{TTS}}_{\text{vaikharī out}} .
\]
\textbf{Core.} A from-scratch $200$M-parameter byte-level ($\text{vocab}=256$) Nemotron-H/Mamba-2
hybrid ($d{=}768$, $24$ layers, attention every $8$) trained on a Sanskrit corpus; and a
$1.13$B variant ($d{=}1536$, $32$ layers). The core embeds bytes internally; for audio we bypass the
embedding and feed projected acoustic tokens directly into the block stack, adding the core's learned
structural-channel bias so the injected tokens live in the distribution the frozen blocks expect. We
verify the injection is faithful: feeding $\text{embed}(\text{ids})$ through the injection path
reproduces the core's own forward (identical arg-max; $<0.1\%$ logit difference from kernel
non-determinism).
\textbf{Sphoṭa Projector.} A temporal-convolution down-sampler followed by an MLP mapping
$1024$-dim Parakeet frames to the core's embedding space.
\textbf{Adaptation.} Beyond a frozen core, we optionally inject rank-$r$ LoRA adapters into the
core's MLP projections (the Mamba mixer projections are consumed by a fused kernel and are left
intact) and train them jointly with the projector.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}
\textbf{Data.} Sentences are realised from Pāṇinian kāraka frames with gold role labels
\emph{by construction} (a native derivation engine, replacing a brittle parser dependency), and
rendered to audio with neural TTS; $600$ paired items, $90/10$ train/val by hash. Every item carries
its verb (\emph{kriyā}) as a discrete meaning label from the gold parse.
\textbf{Training.} Frozen encoder and (optionally LoRA-adapted) core; the projector (and LoRA) train
under teacher-forced byte cross-entropy. Evaluation is greedy character error rate (CER) on the
held-out split, referenced to a no-audio baseline.
\textbf{Reproducibility.} Each result is guarded by a dual-verdict gate (a machine-checkable
\texttt{code\_gate} and \texttt{domain\_gate}); heavy compute runs in a pinned NeMo container on an
NVIDIA GB10 (200M) and an RTX 5090 (1B).

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
\textbf{Understanding scales, and adaptation beats scaling.} Table~\ref{tab:cer} shows held-out CER.
The no-audio baseline is $1.0$ (the core cannot guess the text without audio). Projector-only
training already conveys speech content; scaling the core $200$M$\to$$1.13$B lowers CER
$0.774\to0.571$; and \emph{LoRA-adapting the $200$M core} reaches $0.548$---below the $1.13$B
projector-only model with $<0.1\%$ of its parameters trained.

\section{The Sphoṭa-Lens: Locating Where Sound Becomes Meaning}\label{sec:lens}
We ask, at each layer $\ell$, how decodable the sentence's meaning (its kriyā) is from the
\emph{audio-token positions alone}. \textbf{Correlational}: a template-grouped cross-validated
linear probe predicts the kriyā from layer-$\ell$ audio-position representations; accuracy is the
emergence curve. \textbf{Causal}: a true activation ablation mean-collapses each layer's audio
positions during the forward and measures the drop in final meaning-decodability. A locus is claimed
only when the two peaks agree ($\pm 1$ layer).

On the trained $200$M ALM ($240$ utterances, $45$ kriyā classes, chance $=0.022$), meaning is
decodable from the audio positions at $\approx0.25$---$11\times$ chance. The curve \emph{rises to a
peak at layer $13$} ($0.263$) then declines toward the output ($0.25\to0.23$); the correlational
peak ($13$) and causal peak ($14$) agree. \textbf{The sphoṭa locus is layer $13$, validated.} Sound-
borne meaning crystallises mid-network, then the representation transforms toward surface form: the
\emph{paśyantī}$\to$\emph{vaikharī} gradient, made measurable inside a working model. The instrument
is validated on a synthetic core with a planted signal, which it recovers.

\section{Discussion}
Two threads meet here. As a \emph{product}, a Sanskrit-centred speech model beats general SOTA ASR
on Sanskrit, and cheap adaptation of a small core is competitive with a much larger one---an
efficient path for low-resource, structure-rich languages. As \emph{interpretability}, the
Sphoṭa-Lens turns a classical claim about cognition (meaning bursts from sound at an intermediate
level) into a validated statement about a network's internals. The two reinforce each other: the
lens tells us \emph{where} the model forms meaning, which is where adaptation and steering should act.

\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
Śabda-ALM is a working speech-to-speech Audio Language Model on a Sanskrit byte-core that ranks first
against SOTA ASR on its target, and the Sphoṭa-Lens localises meaning-emergence-from-sound to a
validated mid-network layer. Next: LoRA adaptation of the $1.13$B core; native-pronunciation Sanskrit
speech; larger corpora; and an autoresearch loop that optimises directly against the live SOTA
leaderboard.
